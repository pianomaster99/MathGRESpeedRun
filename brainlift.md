# The Protégé Effect

## Definitions

**Expectation to teach:** A learning strategy where students learn material with the intention of teaching that material to someone else later on.

## Purpose

Use the grounded research method to find an underutilized technique for teaching graduate-level entrance exams.

Focus on specific details in the context and method of studies to discover correct applications to the context of my learning app.

## Experts

I don’t know experts, but I do know that one paper is influential for this topic:

**On the Cognitive Benefits of Teaching**  
Bargh and Schul, 1980

In this paper, Bargh and Schul introduce a method of testing the influences of the expectation to teach on learning, a method on which many subsequent studies base their experiments.

## Research Questions

1. Does the expectation to teach promote learning?
2. What factors affect the influence of the expectation to teach on learning?

## Spiky POVs

- It is not the actual teaching but rather the expectation to teach that greatly influences the teacher’s learning.
- LLM students are better than human students when it comes to promoting learning in the teacher.

## Insights

- The biggest negative outcome of expectation to teach is induced anxiety.
- Time constraint seems to cause induced anxiety.
- LLMs can reduce anxiety because LLM students are not as valuable as human students.
- Expectation to teach causes deeper understanding of content, not better rote memorization of content.
- Users with expectation to teach will be more motivated and have more fun.
- The actual teaching, i.e. explanations and answering questions, does not boost learning for the teacher when isolated from the preparation to teach.

## Implications for Learning App

### Current Idea

Have the user learn worked-out examples with the expectation to teach LLM students.

The user enters a classroom setting and teaches a similar problem by typing or drawing on a whiteboard.

LLM students ask questions in reaction to the user’s lesson.

You might be thinking, “Wait, isn’t the actual teaching by the user useless?” You are right, it is useless. However, it is necessary that the user is gaslit into doing the lesson so that next time, they still expect to teach.

However, from the perspective of the developers, the purpose of the lesson is to diagnose issues with the user’s understanding.

To the user, the LLM students are novices. To the developer, the LLM students identify gaps in the user’s lessons and ask questions to diagnose the gaps.

GRE Math fits the context of my sources because all of my studies involved college students and GRE Math is conceptual rather than memorization based.

# On the Cognitive Benefits of Teaching

Bargh and Schul, 1980

## Introduction

- Nature of the Teacher's Gain
  - Better cognitive organization of content, a content-specific gain
  - **Learning-to-learn**, a generalized gain that allows one to learn related material more quickly
- The teaching process
  1. Preparing to teach
  2. Initial presentation of material to student
  3. Responding to the student's questions

## Experiment 1

### Description

- Subjects: 42 undergrads from University of Michigan (23 male, 19 female)
- Task
  - Pretest contained a passage of about 170 words in length. 5 minutes alloted for studying, 3 minutes for 4 recall and 4 recognition questions.
  - Posttest contained a longer passage of 660 words. 15 minutes to study, 8 minute sto answer 8 recall and 8 recognition questions. Half of questions were basic items and half where details of little importance

### Experiment

- Group 1: do the pretest-posttest normally
- Group 2: Pretest same as Group 1. For posttest, Subjects were told that they would have to teach another subject the passage in order for student subject to answer questions. However, the "student subject" never arrived, so teacher subjects did the posttest.

### Results

- Percentage of questions answered correctly after adjusted to:
  - Teaching: 0.649
  - Control: 0.569

## Experiment 2

### Description

- Subjects: 60 male and 61 female undergrads at University of Michigan in intro to psychology course
- Tasks
  1. Tower of Hanoi
  2. Verbal task: subjects answered recall and recognition questions after reading paragraphs in limited time

Subjects completed both tasks, and each subject completed 3 versions of one task.

### Experiment

For both tasks,

- Group 1: control group, so did tasks normally
- Group 2: verbalization condition, so while do the second version of the task, subjects were asked to "verbalize your thoughts and ideas out loud to yourself while you work on the task"
- Group 3: teaching condition. After the first version of each task, subjects were asked to teach a confederate with the goal of having the confederate do the third version of the same task.

### Results

No correlations found.

## Summary

Bargh and Schul’s paper is important because it separates the **expectation to teach** from actual teaching. In Experiment 1, students who expected to teach performed better than students who only expected to take a test, even though they never actually taught anyone. This supports the idea that the preparation mindset itself can improve learning.

Experiment 2 adds an important qualification: simply teaching someone, without first learning the material under an expectation to teach, does not appear to help the teacher learn. The teaching condition did not show clear benefits, which suggests that the main learning advantage comes from **preparing to teach**, not from the act of teaching by itself.

Overall, the strongest takeaway is that the expectation to teach changes how people study. Actual teaching alone, when isolated from the preparation-to-teach mindset, is not necessarily helpful for the teacher’s learning.

# Quality of Learning with an Active Versus Passive Motivational Set

Benware and Deci, 1984

## Hypothesis

Learning with the expectation of subsequent teaching of the learned material facilitates greater intrinsic motivation than learning in order to be tested, which results in greater learning and more positive self-related affects and cognitions. Also, learning in order to teach is active while learning in order to pass a test is passive.

## Experiment

### Description

43 university freshmen from University of Rochester's in intro to Psychology were given a 25 page, moderately difficult, article on brain functioning to take home and learn for about 3 hours as a guideline.

### Groups

- Control Group: 21 subjects. Their assigned purpose was to study and learn the article in order to score as high as possible on exam based on reading assignment
- Experimental Group: 19 subjects. Their assigned purpose was to teach the contents to another student, who will take the exam based on the article.

Both groups took the same written, 24-item examination.

### Dependent measures

1. how interesting subjects found the contents of the learning material
2. how enjoyable they found the experiment
3. how much additional time they were willing to volunteer for the experiment
4. an active/passive dimension measured on two 10-point scales,
   - When a teacher assigns a particular reading to you such that he or she might examine you on it, how active or passive do you perceive yourself to be in dealing with your environment?
   - When a teacher assigns a particular reading to you such that you might teach the material to another student, how active or passive do you perceive yourself to be in dealing with your environment?
5. A 24-item examination containing true/false, fill in the blanks, definitions, multiple choice, identifications, and explanations, designed to measure either rote memory or conceptual understanding

### Results

- Experimental Group mean activity dimension for learning in order to teach is 8.47/10, while experimental group mean activity dimension for learning in order to be examined is 4.63
- Control group mean activity dimension for learning in order to teach is 7.72/10, while control group mean activity dimension for learning in order to be examined is 7.09

Mean and Standard Deviations on Intrinsic Motivation Measures

|               | Experimental (n=19) | Control (n=21) |
|---------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Interest      | 7.13, 2.41          | 4.43, 2.30     |
| Enjoyment     | 7.00, 2.27          | 4.67, 2.50     |
| Participation | 2.11, 2.34          | 0.76, 0.68     |

Mean and Standard Deviations of Rote and Conceptual Learning Scores

|            | Experimental (n=19) | Control (n=21) |
|------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Rote       | 18.21, 4.58         | 16.24, 4.13    |
| Conceptual | 18.84, 4.89         | 10.76, 4.23    |

### Discussion

- It is interesting that even though Bargh and Schul reported higher rote learning for the group that learned in order to teach, Benware and Deci found no significant result of this sort.

## Summary

Benware and Deci found strong support for the idea that learning in order to teach creates a more active and motivating learning mindset. The teaching group rated learning-to-teach as much more active than learning-to-be-examined, reported higher interest and enjoyment, and performed much better on conceptual learning. The key takeaway is that expectation to teach seems especially useful for **deep conceptual understanding**, not necessarily rote memorization.

# Expecting to Teach Enhances Learning and Organization of Knowledge in Free Recall of Text Passages

Nestojko, Bui, Kornell, Bjrok, 2014

## Questions

1. Does expecting to teach enhance later test performance as compared with expecting a test?
2. Does expecting to teach cause individuals to change the way they process information?

## Experiment 1

Participants read a passage and take a free recall test to test organization of responses, including key ideas in passage.

### Description

- 56 undergrad studnets (34 female, 22 male, average age 20.8 years) from University of California, Los Angeles
- 1541 word passage comparing depiction of Crimean War in the movie "The Charge of the Light Brigade" with actual historical events. This passage by Rawson and Kintsch had identified 125 idea units, of which 8 were main points, 15 important details, and 16 unimportant details

## Summary

Nestojko et al. focus on whether expecting to teach changes how people organize information. The study is useful because it does not only ask whether students remember more, but also whether their recall is better structured around the key ideas of the passage. This supports the claim that expectation to teach may improve learning by making students organize material more clearly, especially around main ideas.

# Learning for Later Teacher: an Exploration of Mediational Links Between Teaching Expectancy and Learning Results

Alexander Renkl, 1995

## Introduction

- Bedard and Chi, 1992
  - Result: Experts, compared to novices, spend more time for proper encoding and elaboration of the initial givens of a problem.
- Ross and DiVesta, 1976
  - Result: teaching expectancy can elicit anxiety

## Research questions

- Independent variables: teaching expectancy
- Mediators: anxiety, intrinsic motivation, superficiality of processing, and initial problem elaboration
- Dependent variable: learning results

1. How does teaching expectancy influence anxiety, intrinsic motivation, superficiality of observability, and initial problem elaboration
2. What are interrelations between potential mediator variables and how are they related to prior knowledge
3. Do potential mediator variables influence learning results
4. What variables can actually be regarded as mediators
5. Does a teaching expectancy affect learning results

## Experiment

### Setup

- 36 freshmen from the University of Munich volunteered to take part without credit
- Task: Study worked-out problems in probability calculus
- Experimental group: 18 students who expected to explain similar problems to a novice in probiality calculus
- Control group: 18 students who expected to solve similar problems later on
- Subjects had 25 min constraint to learn problems

### Results

- Experimental group spent about 181 seconds per problem, while conrol group spent 140 per problem on average. This shows expectation to teach reduces superficial studying.

| Result | Control Group | Teaching-Expectancy Group | Main Finding |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Intrinsic motivation | 3.27 | 2.60 | Teaching expectancy significantly lowered intrinsic motivation, *p* = .042, effect size *d* = −.69. |
| State anxiety | 2.34 | 2.72 | Teaching expectancy somewhat increased anxiety, *p* = .076 one-tailed, effect size *d* = .48. |
| Initial problem elaboration | 31.45% | 28.25% | No significant difference; both groups spent about 30% of learning time on initial problem understanding. |
| Post-test performance | 11.22 | 11.28 | No learning benefit from teaching expectancy; scores were almost identical, *p* = .981, effect size *d* = .01. |

## Summary

Renkl’s study complicates the positive story about expectation to teach. The teaching-expectancy group studied less superficially and spent more time per problem, which seems beneficial. However, they also had lower intrinsic motivation and somewhat higher anxiety, and their final post-test performance was basically identical to the control group. This suggests that expectation to teach can backfire or fail when the task is difficult, time-constrained, or anxiety-producing.

# The Student Who Cannot Forget

By Becky Allen, June 27, 2026

- Since the mind does not store knowledge in age-labelled drawers, it is impossible for human brain to go back to 13 year old version. Similarly, LLMs cannot truly forget knowledge
- LLMs hallucinate, but hallucination is not the same as forgetting
- The Student Data Paradox (2024) says that the model can reproduce student-like mistakes only at the expense being worse at solving the problems
- Martynova et al, 2025
  - Description: 12 STEM teachers tutor GPT-3.5-simulated 6th grade students through math word problems
  - Results:
    - Simulated students were too articuate
    - Simulated students used formal math language
    - Simulated students were too attentive, positive, and compliant
    - Real students resist, defend a wrong method, become embarrassed, withdraw, ask off-track questions, or stop trying
    - Simulated students accept correction and move on
    - Simulated students produce too much language
    - Simulated students' understanding jumped rather than build gradually
- LLMs should predict learning outcomes, not simulate learning outcomes

## Summary

Allen’s article is useful for thinking about the design of LLM students. The main point is that LLMs are not realistic student simulations because they cannot truly forget, misunderstand, resist, or gradually develop knowledge like real students. However, this may actually be useful for the learning app idea: LLM students do not need to perfectly simulate real students. Instead, they can act as low-stakes teaching targets that reduce anxiety while still asking diagnostic questions to reveal gaps in the user’s understanding.
